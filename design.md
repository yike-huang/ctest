# Art-Based Emotional Reflection App for Cancer Patients and Survivors

## 1. Project Vision

This project aims to create a gentle, trustworthy, art-based emotional reflection website for people living with and beyond cancer. The website helps users notice and express their emotional needs through short, personalized, creative activities grounded in reliable psychosocial support resources.

The app is not a diagnostic tool, medical device, crisis service, or replacement for therapy. It is a supportive companion that helps users:

- Record how they feel in a low-burden way.
- Reflect on emotional patterns with care and nuance.
- Receive art-inspired coping activities matched to their current emotional state, energy level, and cancer journey context.
- Access trustworthy psychosocial resources from authoritative cancer and health organizations.
- Know when and how to seek human support.

## 2. Target Users

The primary users are:

- People newly diagnosed with cancer.
- People currently receiving treatment.
- Cancer survivors after active treatment.
- People living with recurrence, metastatic disease, or long-term uncertainty.
- Optionally, caregivers may be supported in a later version, but the first product should focus on patients and survivors.

The website should assume users may have limited emotional, physical, or cognitive energy because of treatment, fatigue, pain, uncertainty, medication effects, or distress.

## 3. Core Design Principles

### 3.1 Gentle, Not Demanding

The product should reduce pressure rather than create another task. Users should be able to complete a check-in or activity in 3 to 10 minutes.

Design implications:

- Use short questions.
- Allow users to skip sensitive fields.
- Avoid streaks, scores, rankings, or productivity-style progress.
- Let users pause, save, or discard reflections.

### 3.2 Supportive, Not Diagnostic

The app may identify emotional signals, but it must not diagnose mental health conditions.

Use:

- "You may be experiencing..."
- "Your entry suggests..."
- "This activity may help you explore..."

Avoid:

- "You have depression."
- "You are clinically anxious."
- "This activity will treat your distress."

### 3.3 Art-Inspired, Not Clinical Art Therapy

The website should describe its activities as art-inspired reflection or creative coping activities. Formal art therapy is usually delivered by trained professionals and should not be implied unless the product is supervised by qualified clinicians.

Use:

- "Creative reflection activity"
- "Art-inspired coping exercise"
- "Guided drawing or color-based reflection"

Avoid:

- "This is art therapy" unless clinically supervised.
- Claims that creative activities can cure or treat cancer-related distress.

### 3.4 Evidence-Aware and Source-Grounded

Recommendations should be supported by reliable sources such as:

- National Cancer Institute (NCI)
- American Cancer Society (ACS)
- Cancer.Net / American Society of Clinical Oncology (ASCO)
- Cancer Support Community
- Major cancer hospitals or academic medical centers
- Peer-reviewed research and systematic reviews

The website should make clear that art-based activities may support emotional expression and coping, but they are not substitutes for medical care, psychological treatment, or crisis support.

### 3.5 Emotionally Careful Language

The tone must be warm, validating, and precise. The app should avoid toxic positivity or language that implies users are responsible for feeling better.

Use:

- "What you are feeling deserves care."
- "You do not have to make sense of everything today."
- "A small exercise is enough."
- "Reaching out to your care team can be a strong next step."

Avoid:

- "Stay positive."
- "Everything happens for a reason."
- "You just need to be strong."
- "Complete this to improve your mindset."

## 4. Product Experience Model

The core flow should be:

**Check in -> Understand -> Create -> Reflect -> Get Support**

This structure gives the website a complete emotional support loop:

1. The user records how they feel.
2. The app reflects possible emotional needs.
3. The app recommends a gentle creative activity.
4. The user completes or adapts the activity.
5. The app helps the user save insights or access support resources.

## 5. Key Website Pages

### 5.1 Welcome / Home

Purpose:

Introduce the website as a calm, supportive emotional reflection space and give users immediate access to the main actions.

Primary actions:

- "Record how I feel today"
- "Start a 5-minute art activity"
- "I need support now"

Content requirements:

- State that the website offers supportive reflection, not medical diagnosis.
- Use a visually calm art-forward design.
- Avoid a marketing-heavy landing page.
- Make the product itself visible in the first screen.

### 5.2 Gentle Check-In

Purpose:

Collect the user's current emotional and contextual state in a low-burden format.

Suggested inputs:

- Main emotion: anxious, angry, sad, numb, lonely, afraid, uncertain, hopeful, tired.
- Emotion intensity: 1 to 5.
- Body energy: low, medium, high.
- Optional diary entry.
- Optional cancer journey stage:
  - Newly diagnosed
  - In treatment
  - After treatment
  - Monitoring or scan waiting
  - Recurrence or metastatic disease
  - Prefer not to say
- Optional cancer type.
- Optional age range.

Design requirements:

- Every sensitive question should be skippable.
- The page should work even if the user only selects one emotion.
- The app should not ask for directly identifying information such as full name, address, medical record number, hospital ID, or exact birthdate.

### 5.3 Emotion Reflection

Purpose:

Translate the user's check-in and diary into a careful, non-diagnostic reflection.

Suggested sections:

- "What seems present today"
- "What you may need"
- "A gentle activity to try"
- "When to seek more support"

Example tone:

> Your check-in suggests anxiety and low energy may be present today. That can happen when treatment, appointments, side effects, or uncertainty take up a lot of space. You do not need to solve the feeling all at once. A short grounding activity may be enough for now.

Safety requirements:

- If the user indicates severe distress, self-harm, hopelessness, or immediate danger, the page should prioritize support resources rather than standard activity recommendations.
- The app should clearly encourage contacting emergency services, a crisis line, a healthcare team, or a trusted person when appropriate.

### 5.4 Activity Recommendations

Purpose:

Recommend personalized art-inspired coping activities based on the user's emotion, intensity, energy level, and cancer journey stage.

Each activity card should include:

- Activity name.
- Emotion it supports.
- Estimated time.
- Energy level required.
- Materials needed.
- Audio guidance option.
- Short explanation of why it may help.
- Related authoritative source or evidence note.

Example activity mapping:

| Emotional Need | Recommended Activity | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Anxiety | Safe Place Drawing | Draw or assemble an image of a place where your body can imagine some safety. |
| Anger | Scribble Release | Use strong lines, pressure, or color to externalize anger without needing words. |
| Sadness | Color Weather Map | Represent the emotional weather of the day using color, shape, and texture. |
| Numbness | Five Senses Texture Drawing | Use simple marks to reconnect with sensory experience. |
| Uncertainty | Circle of Control Collage | Separate what is controllable, influenceable, and outside your control. |
| Body disconnection | Gentle Body Letter | Write or draw a compassionate message to one part of the body. |
| Loneliness | Connection Postcard | Create a postcard to someone supportive, whether or not it is sent. |

### 5.5 Guided Art Studio

Purpose:

Provide the actual guided creative activity.

Required interface elements:

- A simple drawing, writing, or collage workspace.
- Step-by-step guidance.
- Audio play, pause, and replay controls.
- Low-distraction visual layout.
- Option to save, discard, or privately reflect.

Recommended activity structure:

1. Arrive: brief grounding prompt.
2. Choose: select color, shape, word, or material.
3. Create: complete a short expressive action.
4. Notice: observe what changed or stayed the same.
5. Close: save, discard, or choose a next support step.

Design requirements:

- Do not require artistic skill.
- Do not judge the output.
- Do not frame the activity as homework.
- Support very short sessions.

### 5.6 My Reflections

Purpose:

Help users review emotional patterns and saved creative reflections without pressure.

Possible features:

- Recent check-ins.
- Emotion color map.
- Saved artworks or written reflections.
- Activities that felt useful.
- Gentle weekly summary.

Avoid:

- Streaks.
- Performance metrics.
- "Good" or "bad" emotion labels.
- Any implication that recovery depends on using the app consistently.

### 5.7 Resource Library

Purpose:

Provide trustworthy psychosocial resources connected to the user's needs.

Suggested categories:

- Emotions and cancer.
- Anxiety and distress.
- Anger, sadness, fear, and uncertainty.
- Talking with a care team.
- Support groups and counseling.
- Survivorship resources.
- Crisis and urgent support.

Each resource should include:

- Source organization.
- Why it is relevant.
- Link.
- Date accessed or last reviewed when available.

### 5.8 Support and Safety

Purpose:

Give users a clear path to human support when the app is not enough.

This page should be accessible from every screen.

Content should include:

- A statement that the user deserves immediate support if they feel at risk or overwhelmed.
- Encouragement to contact their oncology team, nurse navigator, social worker, mental health professional, or a trusted person.
- For users in the United States, mention 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline for suicidal thoughts, self-harm urges, or emotional crisis.
- Emergency instructions should be adapted by region if the product is international.

Example tone:

> You do not need to handle this moment alone. This website cannot provide emergency care, but real-time support is available. If you may hurt yourself or are in immediate danger, contact emergency services now or reach out to a crisis line in your area.

## 6. Personalization Logic

The recommendation engine should consider:

- Primary emotion.
- Emotion intensity.
- Body energy.
- Diary language.
- Cancer journey stage.
- User preferences.
- Past activities that the user found helpful.

Basic rule examples:

- High anxiety + low energy -> short grounding, color breathing, safe place drawing.
- Anger + medium/high energy -> scribble release, tearing paper collage, strong-line drawing.
- Sadness + low energy -> color weather map, small memory object drawing.
- Numbness + low energy -> sensory texture exercise.
- Scan waiting + uncertainty -> circle of control collage.
- Body discomfort + sadness -> gentle body letter or body outline with compassionate color.

The app should prioritize low-effort recommendations when energy is low.

## 7. Safety and Escalation Rules

The app should detect or allow user selection of severe distress signals, including:

- Thoughts of self-harm.
- Desire to disappear or not wake up.
- Feeling unsafe.
- Panic that feels unmanageable.
- Severe hopelessness.
- Inability to function or cope.

When these signals appear:

- Do not continue with normal recommendations as the only response.
- Show supportive, direct language.
- Encourage immediate human support.
- Provide crisis and emergency resources.
- Offer to help the user identify one person or care team contact to reach.

The app should not attempt to counsel the user through a crisis by itself.

## 8. Visual and Interaction Direction

The design should feel:

- Calm.
- Human.
- Artful.
- Trustworthy.
- Accessible.
- Mature rather than childish.

Recommended visual language:

- Soft neutral background with carefully used color accents.
- Paper, canvas, ink, watercolor, or colored pencil textures.
- Clear typography and generous spacing.
- High contrast for readability.
- Minimal decorative clutter.

Avoid:

- Hospital-like coldness.
- Overly cheerful wellness branding.
- Heavy gradients or decorative blobs.
- Gamified badges or achievement visuals.
- Visuals that imply cure, battle, victory, or failure.

Accessibility requirements:

- Large readable text.
- Clear buttons.
- Keyboard navigability.
- Screen reader labels.
- Audio guidance with text alternative.
- Low-motion options.
- Mobile-first support.

## 9. Content and Tone Guidelines

Preferred language:

- "You can stop at any time."
- "Only answer what feels okay."
- "This does not need to look beautiful."
- "Your reaction makes sense in a hard situation."
- "A small step counts."
- "If this feels too heavy, support from a real person may help."

Language to avoid:

- "Stay positive."
- "Fight harder."
- "Beat cancer with mindset."
- "Do not worry."
- "Everything will be fine."
- "You failed to complete your activity."

## 10. Evidence and Source Strategy

The website should maintain a source library and connect recommendations to credible evidence.

Primary sources to use:

- National Cancer Institute. "Coping with Cancer." https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/coping
- National Cancer Institute. "Emotions and Cancer." https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/coping/feelings
- National Cancer Institute. "Adjustment to Cancer: Anxiety and Distress." https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/coping/feelings/anxiety-distress-pdq
- American Cancer Society. "Managing Distress." https://www.cancer.org/cancer/managing-cancer/side-effects/emotional-mood-changes/distress/managing-distress.html
- American Cancer Society. "Therapy, Counseling, and Support Resources for People with Cancer." https://www.cancer.org/cancer/survivorship/coping/understanding-psychosocial-support-services.html
- Creative arts therapy systematic review: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13000673/

Evidence wording should be cautious:

- "Research suggests creative arts therapies may help some cancer patients with anxiety, depression, and quality of life."
- "These exercises are supportive reflection tools, not treatment."
- "If distress is persistent, severe, or affecting daily life, users should contact a healthcare professional."

## 11. Privacy and Data Principles

Because the app handles sensitive health-adjacent information, privacy should be designed from the start.

Principles:

- Collect the minimum information needed.
- Make sensitive fields optional.
- Do not collect direct identifiers unless absolutely necessary.
- Explain how diary entries are used.
- Allow users to delete entries.
- Avoid sharing data with third parties without explicit consent.
- Store user-generated emotional data securely if accounts are introduced.

Initial prototype recommendation:

- Use local-only storage or clearly marked demo data.
- Do not require account creation for the first experience.

## 12. MVP Scope

The first usable website should include:

- Welcome page.
- Gentle check-in flow.
- Emotion reflection result page.
- Activity recommendation page.
- At least 5 guided art-inspired activities.
- Resource library with authoritative sources.
- Support and safety page.
- Simple local saving of reflections, if feasible.

MVP activities:

1. Safe Place Drawing.
2. Scribble Release.
3. Color Weather Map.
4. Circle of Control Collage.
5. Gentle Body Letter.

## 13. Future Enhancements

Potential later features:

- Audio narration for every activity.
- User preference learning.
- Clinician-reviewed activity library.
- Multilingual support.
- Printable activity sheets.
- Integration with hospital psychosocial support referrals.
- Optional caregiver mode.
- Secure account-based reflection history.
- More nuanced cancer-stage-specific resources.

## 14. Success Criteria

The website is successful if users can:

- Understand what the app does within 30 seconds.
- Complete a check-in with minimal effort.
- Receive a recommendation that feels emotionally relevant.
- Start an art-inspired activity without needing artistic skill.
- Feel respected rather than judged.
- Find reliable support resources.
- Know what to do if their distress feels urgent or unsafe.
